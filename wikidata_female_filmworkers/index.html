Finding Data about Female Contributors to Film Productions [1]
A common assumption about the German "Kleberinnen" was, that they had a low income and they were regarded as low ranking within the film producing industry. Going by oral testimonies, that might be rather a myth, probably based on a contemporary perspective, which is influenced by the "auteur cinema" in which the director is the only creative (thus: influential) person. In "Die kleinen Kleberinnen" the elderly women talk animately about their career, their craft and about the pride they still feel a valued and well paid film workers. Even though they had signed contracts (e.g. for the Decla, a German production studio which existed before the Ufa), they worked for other studios as well, ands sometimes even from their own homes, thus increasing their salaries even further. Because they were needed as experienced and skillful negative cutters, nobody really challenged that practice. In the same interviews, the women stress that gluing film was by far not the only job they knew, if necessary, they also printed and developed film for their needs.

Enriching our traditional filmographies and enhancing our knowledge about historical (and contemporary) filmproduction has certainly been put on the agenda of film scholars and film historians worldwide. In the last couple of years pivotal books were published about female employees in Hollywood (see Smyth) or women in British film production (see Bell), research projects like STUDIOTEC: Film Studios: Infrastructure, Culture, Innovation in Britain, France, Germany and Italy, 1930-60 and generally research which challenges the notion of a predominantly make and national film history by taking emigrés and female filmworkers into account. These publications have used archival and private sources as well as oral history thus developing a different methodology for their research needs and questions unanswered by known records. They show that "the myths surrounding women's work as unskilled or biologically determined have been compounded by established film historiographies" and need to be challenged by unearthing more data (see Bell, 2021: 6). The "cherished notion in Western culture of the romantic artist as individual genius" (see ibid.) is being discussed which may have huge implications on how we credit authorship in our databases. Some go as far as challening the whole structure: "Perhaps asking who should get credit for one or another part of this integrated creative activity is asking the wrong question. What we need to be asking is: what is creative thought in a distributed cognitive system?" (Pearlman, 2018b). Studies like these stress that looking at female careers needs a different methodology as well, because the norm of "continous work histories" with an ongoing filmmaking record in one studio can't be applied here to assess success in the traditiponal sense (see Bell, 2021: 7). As Bell stresses, many women's working lives "were shaped by child care and domestic responsibilities", resulting in "episodic waves of working, where carreer and family alternatively" took center stage (see ibid.). For her, the absence of women in film history need to be addressed with a fitting methodology, where first of all, the concept of the episodic (interrupted) carreer has to serve as a lense "through which to recover women's occupational labor". Secondly, women's achievements need to be contextualized within a climate of discrimination, and lastly, a more flexible, inclusive model of creativity "to accomodate the many and varied tasks women undertoook in the performance of their professional duties in below-the-line roles" (see Bell, 2021: 8). Of course we need to take national and studio traditions into account and a lot more work is to be done here in order to be able to write a more comprehensive history of women in film production. sometimes the careers in different countries and studios were exactly the opposite: while women only slowly got more significant roles in the British film and television industry in the 1950s and 1960s, they started to disappear from important positions from the Hollywood studios at the end of the 1950s due to the decline of the studio system (see Smyth 2018: 11).

One persistant myth is arguable, that women just were not present in early film production and therefore we don't know their names. Thanks to scholars like Smyth we now know that for example Hollywood women actually had played an major part, with many of them having influential positions and able to bring their vision and creativity on to the screen. Other scholars look into Russian film history and particularly unearth early female film producers and cinema owners (Drubek 2021, Kovalova and Bagrov 2021). Smyth argues, that it is not some sort of law of nature that these names disappear from film history: "However much Hollywood acted as an advocate for working women during the heyday of the studio system, male historians tended to edit out women's roles, leaving them on the cutting room floor of conventional film histories" (see Smyth 2018: 13). She quotes authors like Ramsaye, Hampton, Jacobs, Rosten and Knight, who "all focused on a progressive historical model founded on technological, artistic, and commercial innovation dominated by a male cast of producers and directors" (see ibid.). Drubek argues in similar ways about actively leaving out film workers who were not Russian, but had an emigrant background. More and thorough research is needed to get a more comprehensive picture on how film historiography was written for different countries and marginalized groups, but maybe there is a parallel with how history is generally written by the dominant group and based on patriarchal and capitalistic notions of success and creativity - as Bell points out. Challenges our view on national cinema as well as on only female figures being written out of history. It is rather a erased film history of "Us-Them'ness" (Quote from "Behavior). Would be inclined to call on an explanation which is based on human nature rather than on conscious power play, but that needs to be investigated further.

How to find data on female filmworkers? Were they actively forgotten? Were they ever recorded? Where should we start looking? As Sarah-Mai Dang points out, that the awareness of missing sources has become a key issue of feminist debate: "How can we identify and include blind spots when trying to reconstruct the past? In what way can we narrate ellipsis and absences while avoiding the pitfall of implicitly promising to grasp the "whole" story once enough information will be gathered? How is it possible to explore uncharted territory when faced with the lack of historical objects?" (Dang, 2020). On the other hand, more sources than ever, have been digitised and put online. There is an imminent risk that by focussing on online sources, we neglect the plentitude of diverse and most likely unrecorded offline sources (Dang, 2020), like for example information which stems from viewing the moving images itself which many times have not been preserved and made accessible. In short, only by joining forces between archivists and researchers by creating more sources while analysing the information (for example deciphering pseudonyms, which were used by women many times), can we gather more data for our explorations and draw conclusions about the involvement of female filmworkers.

Women in film history are not easy to locate in film archives' databases and available filmographies for several reasons. Not many databases distinguish their cast and credits information into at least a binary distinction of male/female. And if there was the option, it arguably up until some years ago didn't feel as something mandatory anyway. What would a film archive do with such information? The project BFI Filmography provided a novel view on filmographic data by considering their metadata as something which can and should be mined and analysed as a valuable corpus of data. Probably not many outside of film archives can understand how pioneering (and costly) this project was. It was a political and strategic decision to dedicate resources (human and technical) to enriching their data with gender information by combining the film archives' data (in that case prenames) with "available gendered" prenames on the internet. Only then the BFI was able to show the gender distribution in British film production. A process like this one can't be without errors, but it supports intellectual, manual work by catalogers.

There is no space to elaborate more on this, but it is worth stressing, that "data is trimmed or transfigured to match the expectations of the machine", which can be called the "schematic bias" (Thorp 2021: 131):

For those involved in building data systems, a schema is a kind of blueprint, a map of which types of information will be stored, in what form, and which types of information will be rejected. In cognitive science, a schema is a pattern of thought, a framework of preconceived ideas that directs how a person sees the world: if you observe something that fits neatly into your schema, it gets files easily and efficiently into your memory. On the other hand, schema-foreign things will often not be noticed or remembered, or they will be modified to fit into what you expect based on the frameworks you constructed. (ibid: 132)

We need to keep these thoughts in mind when talking about how we organise information, what we leave out for several reasons and where we can find to-date missing information.
Collecting and working with Data [2]
Databases of film archives are - alas - not always the most available or accessible sources. Another caveat is, that many times cataloguers either take their cast and credits information from secondary sources (which should not be sneered at, because it also can complete fragmented physical elements) or have to fit the data visible on the print/file itself into a quite rigid information system. Complementary action would involve viewing films, especially those at the fringes of industrial film production, like documentary, "Kulturfilme", amateur films, experimental films etc. Another option would be to perform formal analysis to find out more about stylistic conventions, studio style, or even detecting gendered styles to enrich existing data. These highly speculative, but nevertheless fascinating investigations might back up long standing film theories on the female involvement. They might even shatter our understanding of how to even describe a "female" style? We can test our hypothesis on what we would like to uncover/find if we pursue the quest for female names in film production: Do we think that women had a unique voice? Is their contribution distinguishable from the contribution of their male co-workers? Or do we rather just want to close knowledge gaps, no matter of male or female? As film archivists our preliminary answer might be - again - a pragmatic one for the beginning. Collecting existing data would serve as a first step to connect already digitised sources and by comparing and mapping them, complete some records at least.

The pionieering publications mentioned earlier relied on data sources which were mostly mined manually. It is worth asking how the unearthed names and pieces of information are again stored for others to analyse and use for their research questions. These infrastructures within academia rarely exist. The ["Women's Pioneer Project"(https://wfpp.columbia.edu/)] offers a multitude of information, albeit in the form of essays. If we, however, would need to query, compare and visualise the existing data, we need something more similar to a database. Of course, as mentioned above and as it is summarized elsewhere (Dang 2020), databases are not neutral collections of knowledge.

Dang points out, that there is an increasing risk "of letting offline sources be consigned to oblivion", because of the easily and immediately available sources on the Internet. There are many data collections which are not digitised yet and data, which especially for our topic can only be created by scholars to be later (or never) provided online. Valuable data can be found in:
  • archives' databases and cataloguing notes not yet transferred
  • Filmographic sources and other aggregated online databases like Wikidata
  • historical film journals
  • memoirs und interviews
  • studio archival collections, guild reports, trade papers, local reportage, nationally syndicated journalism (Symth)
  • Press publication
  • trade union records (Bell)
  • etc.
For this article we will focus on one online resource and evaluate what is available online today on Wikidata, and potentially analyse where the gaps of data are. We will focus on female film workers and particulary ono data on female editors. Before we do that, let's have a look on how gender is assigned in Wikidata and how reliable this information is.
Classification Male/Female and How Wikidata deals with this [3]
One word on the classification into male/female, which is at best simplistic.

Many twentieth-century feminist scholars attempted to address the social construction of gender by treating gender as something separate from sex. But that distinction is increasingly breaking down. Both gender and sex are social constructs, as it turns out. Even sex, which today is sometimes still considered in biologically essential terms, has a distinct cultural history. It can be traced to a place (Europe) and a time (the Enlightenment) when new theories about democracy and what philosophers called "natural rights" began to emerge. Before then, there was a hierarchy of the sexes, with men on the top and women on the bottom. (Thanks, Aristotle!) But there wasn’t exactly a binary distinction between those two (or any other) sexes. In fact, according to historian of sex and gender Thomas Laqueur, most people believed that women were just inferior men, with penises located inside instead of outside of their bodies and that—for reals!—could descend at any time in life." (from: https://data-feminism.mitpress.mit.edu/pub/h1w0nbqp/release/3)

There is no doubt that the subject is complex, however, if we want to take a first, pragmatic approach in order to at least start the process of analysis, the binary classification can be helpful. Pragmatic because in many cases, we won’t find more differentiated data. If the archives' databases or any other data sources have any gender classification at all, the legacy data is in most cases binary. Even if one wants to enrich data afterwards, e.g. by assigning first names to a gender via external sources, the result remains an approximation to a certain degree. The best answer to overcoming the binary bias would be to have film workers classify themselves, like it it is possible for example in Wikidata already.

Concepts, our view of the world, etc. is very often "datafied", turned into classifications, ontologies and often only binary options (male/female). Even if there is a "other" option, statistics and further processing doesn't work as well. "'What gets counted counts,' feminist geographer Joni Seager has asserted, and Munir is one person who understands that. What is counted—like being a man or a woman—often becomes the basis for policymaking and resource allocation." "The process of converting qualitative experience into data can be empowering, and even has the potential to be healing, as we address toward the end of this chapter. When thoughtfully collected, quantitative data can be empowering too." Both from: https://data-feminism.mitpress.mit.edu/pub/h1w0nbqp/release/3
